## Outline of the Treatment Progress Measure

The Treatment Progress Measure has been developed to give a granular view of
progress being made during substance misuse treatment. The measure includes
information from treatment reviews as well as treatment exits, to give a
picture of people who are still in treatment as well as people who have
completed it. Only the headline figure, the treatment progress percentage for
a local authority or other area, is included in this release. The detailed
categories are available to commissioners and providers of substance misuse
treatment.

As with most National Drug Treatment Monitoring System (NDTMS) reporting, the
measure refers to each individual's status in their latest treatment journey
in the rolling 12 month period. A treatment journey often consists of
multiple overlapping episodes of treatment at different services; you can
find more information on the methodology of treatment journeys in the NDTMS
annual statistics quality and methodology document. The measure applies only
to adults (everyone aged 18 and over) who are in treatment in community
alcohol and drug treatment services (including those aged 18 and over in
young person's services, residential rehabilitation and inpatient
detoxification services). The measure does not use information from NDTMS in
secure settings such as prisons.

When calculating the measure, people in treatment are classified as showing
substantial progress if they meet one of the following criteria:

- **Successfully completed treatment** – achieving a successful completion
  from treatment in the latest reporting period; or
- **Not using problem substances** – are still in treatment and are not using
  their problem substances (see *Problem substances* below); or
- **Substantially reduced use of problem substances** – are still in
  treatment and have substantially reduced use of their problem substances
  (see *Substantially reduced use of problem substances* below).

The full set of categories is shown in table 1.

| Category | Sub-category |
|---|---|
| Showing substantial progress | Successfully completed treatment |
| Showing substantial progress | Not using problem substances |
| Showing substantial progress | Substantially reduced use of problem substances |
| Not currently showing substantial progress | Unplanned exit |
| Not currently showing substantial progress | Completed treatment but with an acute housing problem |
| Not currently showing substantial progress | Not substantially reduced use of problem substances |
| Missing data | Completed treatment but exit TOP data missing |
| Missing data | Still in treatment but TOP/journey data missing |
| New client | Still in treatment, <= 3 months in treatment with insufficient data for classification |

Table 1: Detailed categories of the Treatment Progress Measure

## Reporting the Treatment Progress Measure

The Treatment Progress Measure is calculated as the percentage of those in
the *Showing substantial progress* category, out of the total of those in the
*Showing substantial progress* and *Not currently showing substantial
progress* categories. Those in the *Missing data* and *New client* categories
are excluded from the calculation, because the data isn't available to place
them in either progress category.

The standard in social research is for 80% of data to not be missing to avoid
unacceptable bias. Commissioners and providers of substance misuse treatment
receive reports on their level of missing data. This is not included in the
public release, but over 90% of local authorities and all regions exceed the
80% threshold as of September 2024.

Due to small numbers in treatment, Rutland and the City of London have been
excluded from this release.

Collection of treatment reviews changed at the start of the financial year
2023-24, so figures reported for earlier periods than 2023-24 are not fully
comparable with figures from 2023-24 onwards.

## Further methodological details

### Problem substances

People in substance misuse treatment can record more than one problem
substance. When determining if someone is not using or has reduced their
problem substances, only the primary problem substance, opiates, and crack
are considered.

### Substantially reduced use of problem substances

The established methodology for reliable change is based on change in use
from start of treatment to 6-month review. For this to apply to the whole
treatment population, including those who have been in treatment for a long
period, a 12-month rolling baseline period is used. To count as having
reduced use by a substantial amount, the reduction must be greater than or
equal to the number of days threshold calculated by the established reliable
change index method. This method provides a number of days out of 28 (as
measured at treatment reviews) for the service user to have reduced use by to
be statistically significant, for instance someone who only uses alcohol must
have reduced by 9 days or more out of 28 to count as substantially reduced.
